Section 1 — The core tension

The Engagement / Autonomy Paradox

Every company building AI for children faces one structural pressure: maximize engagement. The optimal outcome for a child is the exact opposite: growing autonomy. These are not reconcilable through good intentions.

Autonomy

Key insight
The missing piece is a context engine. No AI is safe or beneficial for a child per se — outcomes depend entirely on developmental context: the child's age, cognitive state, maturity, and relationship to adults. The field needs independent infrastructure that understands this context and measures outcomes independently of the tools producing them.

Section 3 — How we respond

How can we ensure AI is safe for children and genuinely good for their development?

Four principles guide the answer — and shape the design of every project in this portfolio.

1 · Humans are all you need

The most robust evidence in developmental science points to one variable above all others: the presence of a responsive human being. Human mentorship, parental attunement, peer relationships, trusted adults — these predict outcomes more reliably than any technology ever measured.

This is not sentimentalism. The most rigorous findings in AI in Education consistently show that AI has meaningful impact only when humans remain in the loop — as teachers, mentors, or parents who interact with the output. Remove the human, and effect sizes collapse. The implication is a design principle, not a disclaimer: every AI intervention for children should be designed to strengthen human relationships, not substitute for them.

2 · The personalization opportunity

Benjamin Bloom's 1984 study suggested that one-on-one tutoring could move a child from the 50th to the 98th percentile — a result whose exact magnitude remains debated, but whose direction has held up across decades of replication. For forty years, the question it raised was one we couldn't answer: how do you give every child that kind of attention? Personalizing learning at scale was structurally impossible.

AI breaks this constraint. For the first time, it is technically feasible to give every child — regardless of geography, family income, or language — access to a learning experience adapted to their specific knowledge state, pace, and interests. Not a better average lesson. A lesson designed for this child, today, at this exact stage. The opportunity is not to replace teachers, but to eliminate the universality constraint that makes mass education so inefficient.

3 · The causal database challenge

What separates AI that personalizes superficially from AI that genuinely serves a child's development is not model size or compute. It is the context engine: the infrastructure that understands where a child actually is developmentally — their cognitive state, their specific knowledge gaps, their emotional availability, their relationship to the adult in the room.

This is the unsolved problem. Solve it, and every application in this portfolio becomes dramatically more effective. Without it, AI tutors remain sophisticated autocomplete — responsive to input, blind to the child.

But context alone is not enough. A context engine without the right content produces nothing. And content without a measurable outcome is just activity. What the field actually needs is the full causal loop: context → content → outcome — where the outcome signal comes from outside the model that produced the intervention. Models need an independent reward signal, measured in the real world. Actually, models need millions of those reward signals.

4 · Safety is a process

We cannot safety-test AI for children the way we test a pharmaceutical. There is no placebo group, no 10-year trial before deployment, no regulatory body with the evidence base to approve or reject at scale. AI for children is being deployed in real time, on real children, with near-zero causal understanding of its effects.

The answer is not to wait for perfect knowledge — it is to make safety a property of the ecosystem, not the product. Mandatory developmental crash tests before deployment. Independent measurement infrastructure. Disclosure of cognitive and autonomy impacts. Mechanisms that align commercial incentives with developmental outcomes. Everything that improves the process — oriented toward scale and product — is more valuable than any static safety label. Safety is not a destination, it is a process.

#1 Open Evidence + Context Engine for EducationThe personalization infrastructure the entire field is missing ▾

We need an infrastructure layer to scale learning outcomes.

The causal database: the first global system linking pedagogical interventions to independently measured learning outcomes (external reward signal).

The child context engine: a real-time developmental model of each child — across all spheres of development, because learning outcomes rely on children's holistic development.

#2 The Human BaselineDocumenting the last pre-AI generation — before the record disappears ▾

For the first time in history, machines can not only serve humans — they can genuinely interact with them. Large language models blur the boundary between machine and human in ways that are probably irreversible. We are at the last moment when it is still possible to document, rigorously, how children grow, learn, and form relationships in a world not yet saturated by conversational AI.

Longitudinal ethnographic and neuroscientific documentation of the last pre-AI generation: their cognitive patterns, relational structures, developmental milestones, learning processes. Combined with the first cohort of children growing up with AI from birth — the comparison that defines what actually changed.

This is about establishing the scientific baseline that future researchers, policymakers, and educators will need to understand what the LLM shift actually did to human development. Irreplaceable once this generation is gone.

Core outcomeA permanent open archive: the developmental standard of childhood before and during the LLM transition. The reference point for all future research on what changed.
#4 The Developmental Impact LabelThe Yuka for children's AI — independent, open, binding ▾

Every food product now carries a nutrition label. Every drug has a package insert. AI products targeting children carry nothing. The Developmental Impact Label is an independent institution that evaluates AI products — apps, tutors, companions, games — against a standardized developmental framework, and publishes the results publicly.

The institutional mission goes further: raise public awareness on the developmental risks of unexamined AI design choices, and create structured pressure on AI labs to increase transparency about how their products affect children.

OutcomeAn independent label that becomes the industry standard for child-facing AI. A public pressure mechanism that makes developmental transparency a competitive requirement.
Safety & Agency
#4 Safety by DesignTraining AI models the way humans develop moral judgment ▾

Current AI models capture two of the three pillars of human cognitive development: curiosity-driven exploration (prediction error as learning signal) and progressive compression (efficient representation of regularities). The third pillar is missing: co-regulatory calibration by a more experienced agent — the teacher, the parent, the caregiver. RLHF is a pale approximation — post-hoc, decontextualized, unidirectional. It is feedback, not co-regulation.

Safety by Design funds research into training architectures that incorporate this third pillar — using developmental scaffolding frameworks as a structural constraint during training, not a filter applied after.

OutcomeA scientifically grounded alternative to post-hoc alignment. If it works: the most important safety contribution in the portfolio.
#5 Developmental Browser AgentReal-time AI guardian — protecting development, not just blocking content ▾

A browser extension driven by an AI agent that monitors a child's online experience in real time — not according to static rules or parent-configured blocklists, but according to the child's current developmental stage. When a child attempts to access content, the agent assesses the request in context, can invite the child to explain their intent, evaluates the developmental appropriateness, and decides to allow, filter, or flag.

#6 Adversarial PlaygroundMassively popular games where winning = beating AI ▾

A game studio entirely dedicated to creating massively popular experiences (Minecraft-scale) where the central mechanic is detecting, circumventing, and outperforming AI systems. Playing = building cognitive immunity.

OutcomeA generation that understands AI from the inside — as engineers, not passive consumers.
#7 I'm Stronger Than ChatGPTAnnual global contest — children who made AI work for them ▾

An annual global competition celebrating children who demonstrate genuine mastery of AI — not passive users, but builders, critics, and inventors. Winning entries are things nobody expected a child to make with AI: a tool that solves a real local problem, a piece of music requiring deep compositional intent, a scientific investigation that used AI to surface a real insight.

The judging criterion is simple: did the child make the AI work for them, or did the AI work instead of them? The cultural counter-narrative to AI dependency. Where Adversarial Playground trains cognitive immunity, this contest celebrates it publicly. Translated into 50+ languages, broadcast on national TV in partner countries.

OutcomeA generation that sees AI mastery — not AI use — as the benchmark. A cultural shift no regulation can produce.
#8 The Long GameA cultural endowment — 50 year horizon ▾

Fund artists, novelists, filmmakers, and philosophers to create major cultural works on what it means to be human in the age of AI. Like NASA funded the imagination of space, this fund finances the imagination of human power.

OutcomeIn 30 years, today's children will remember the stories they were told about who they could become.
#12 Human-Centered AI LiteracyKnowing yourself first — then understanding the machine ▾

AI literacy programs today teach children how AI works. This one starts with who the child is. The curriculum combines developmental psychology and computer science — not as two separate disciplines, but as a single integrated lens: understanding AI is inseparable from understanding yourself.

Three pillars, taught from primary school through secondary: Self-knowledge — what are my cognitive strengths, my learning style, my emotional patterns? Human-machine comparison — where do I genuinely outperform AI, and where does AI genuinely outperform me? Not to compete, but to orient. Metacognitive practice — developing an ongoing, reflective relationship with AI use: am I thinking with it or instead of it? Is this making me more capable or more dependent?

The goal is not to produce children who distrust AI. The goal is children who have a stable enough sense of their own intelligence to engage with AI from a position of strength — as an instrument, not a mirror.

OutcomeAn open curriculum deployed in schools across 10 countries. Measurable improvement in metacognitive scores and AI agency benchmarks.
Outcomes & Equity
#10 Neuro-Childhood ObservatoryWhat we don't yet know — and must ▾

The largest longitudinal study ever on AI's neurological impact on developing brains. 50,000 children followed for 20 years: fMRI, cognition, social behavior, attention, creativity, emotional regulation. Open data.

Every current decision — regulatory, product, educational — is made in complete scientific darkness. This is the only project that can provide the evidence base everything else requires.

OutcomeScientific foundation for 20 years of public policy. Without this, we regulate blind.
#11 Human Mentors at ScaleFunded by a levy on AI companies — not from philanthropy alone ▾

A complete reversal of the usual AI logic: use the budget to pay and support human mentors. Compensate qualified adults — craftspeople, scientists, artists, entrepreneurs — to work with children ages 10–18 on real, ambitious projects over 2 years. AI handles logistics, matching, and outcome tracking. The message: human relationship is the scarcest developmental resource.

Funding model: a levy on AI companies proportional to their share of interactions with minors — redistributed as mentor stipends. Same logic as the carbon tax: those who extract value from the resource contribute to replenishing it. Phase 1 is philanthropic; Phase 2 becomes a state-level instrument.

Outcome2 years of real-world project mentorship. 10-year tracking of professional and civic trajectories. A replicable funding model for governments.
#12 Equity TutorOne-on-one Bloom-level tutoring — offline, no subscription, on any phone ▾

Bloom (1984) showed that individualized tutoring dramatically outperforms classroom instruction — the precise effect size is debated, but the direction is not. For 40 years we couldn't scale this. AI is the first technology that can give every child on Earth a Bloom-level tutor.

A lightweight AI tutor (1–3B parameters, 4-bit quantized) deployable on a basic Android phone — no internet required, no subscription, no recurring compute cost. Runs fully offline. Designed for children who have never had access to any tutor at all: rural areas, conflict zones, low-connectivity regions.

OutcomeMeasurable reduction in the achievement gap in 10 countries. Metric: independent test scores — not platform performance.
#13 Radically Simplify Our Understanding of Human DevelopmentImproving parents empowerment ▾

Raising a child is probably the most difficult job on earth, yet no parent receives appropriate training. Every parent has a gem in their hands but has no idea how to carve it. The reason is that developmental science is too complicated — age-dependent, constantly shifting, and extremely subtle to apply at any meaningful level of mastery.

Beyond real-time AI advice tools (which Open Evidence can fuel), there is something more powerful we can do: radically simplify our understanding of human development. Make the science understandable and actionable. After years of thinking about this — including training in newborn observation to understand what is there developmentally from the very beginning — I have a theory worth testing.

OutcomeEvery parent can return to 3 simple principles that radically change how they see their children — and how they can support them.
#14 Radio EducationThe only vector reaching 300M children with no device ▾

300 million children have no smartphone, no tablet, no laptop — but most live within range of a radio signal. Radio is the only educational vector that requires nothing from the recipient. One broadcast, one transmitter, infinite reach.

Use AI to do what radio has never been able to do: generate locally adapted, curriculum-aligned educational content at scale, in any language, updated weekly. AI handles script generation, voice synthesis, and local adaptation. Partner stations in 40 countries carry the content. Teachers get a printed companion guide so classroom follow-up requires no device. Evaluated by independent learning assessments.

OutcomeQuality educational content reaching children in the last mile — no device, no electricity, no subscription. The lowest cost-per-child in the portfolio.
Human Potential
#14 The Polymath EngineAgainst early hyper-specialization ▾

The education system pushes children toward a specialization track by age 14. At exactly the moment when AI is making narrow specialization economically fragile, we are narrowing children faster than ever.

An AI curriculum designed to do the opposite: structured, scaffolded exposure to radically different domains across the full secondary period. A student who spent the morning on differential equations spends the afternoon on Renaissance painting — and an AI draws explicit connections between the two. The hypothesis: in a world where AI handles depth, human value shifts to breadth, synthesis, and the ability to see patterns across domains. The polymath is the most defensible cognitive profile of the next 50 years.

Piloted in 20 schools across 5 countries. Curriculum open-source. Evaluated on creative problem-solving, cross-domain transfer, and long-term professional trajectory.

OutcomeA measurable increase in cross-domain reasoning. An open curriculum any school can adopt. Evidence that breadth and depth are not in conflict.
#14 AI-Native School Network30 schools · 6 continents · one shared experiment ▾

A global network of 30 schools across 6 continents — that commit to a single shared experiment: redesign learning from the ground up as if AI had always existed. Shared principles: spaces designed per neuroscience (natural light, movement, deep-work zones, acoustic control), mandatory AI curriculum feeding data to the portfolio's Context Engine, genuine pedagogical freedom for each school beyond that.

Each school is an independent laboratory. Some will go project-based, others Socratic, others studio-model. The shared infrastructure is the AI curriculum, the data, and the architecture principles. In 10 years the network generates 30 different models of what AI-native education can look like — evidence, not ideology.

Outcome30 proven AI-native school models across 6 continents. Open curriculum. A decade of comparative data on what works, what doesn't, and why.

This portfolio is a starting point, not a ceiling. There are many more project ideas worth pursuing — most of them don't scale in the way the 16 above do, but some of the most important work never does. Mentorship programs, local experiments, policy interventions, community-level initiatives: the non-scalable is often where the deepest insight lives.

One thread runs through all of it: the most robust evidence we have for what supports human development points to other humans. Relationships, presence, attunement — not technology. Continuing to fund programs entirely free of tech is not a retreat from ambition. It is a mitigation policy in its own right, and probably one of the most defensible investments in the portfolio.
